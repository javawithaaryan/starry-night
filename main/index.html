Introduction

Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890) emerged as one of history’s most iconic and influential artists. Though his life was marked by poverty, mental illness, and tragedy, Van Gogh left behind a legacy of over 2,100 artworks and hundreds of letters, reshaping the course of modern art.

1. Early Life and Beginnings

Born in Zundert, Netherlands, Van Gogh was raised in a religious family. He worked as an art dealer, teacher, and missionary before seriously turning to art in his late twenties. Early influences included realism, Dutch painters, and rural themes.

  • The Potato Eaters (1885) — depicts peasant life's hardship.
  • Peasant Woman Digging Up Potatoes (1885)
  • Still Life with Bible (1885)

2. The Paris Revolution (1886–1888)

In Paris Van Gogh encountered Impressionism and avant-garde painters. He adopted brighter colors, looser brushwork, and began painting still lifes, portraits, and city scenes.

  • Self-Portrait as a Painter (1888)
  • Garden with Courting Couples: Square Saint-Pierre (1887)
  • Bouquet of Flowers in a Vase (1887)
  • Vase with Hollyhocks (1886)

3. Breakthrough in Arles (1888–1889)

Seeking tranquility, Van Gogh moved to Arles. His art flourished; he developed a unique color palette and produced many celebrated works.

  • Sunflowers (1889)
  • The Bedroom (1888)
  • The Yellow House (1888)
  • The Langlois Bridge (1888)
  • The Sower (1888)

4. Saint-Rémy Period (1889–1890)

Van Gogh voluntarily entered a mental hospital in Saint-Rémy. Despite struggles, this was a prolific period filled with visionary landscapes.

  • The Starry Night (1889)
  • Wheatfield with a Reaper (1889)
  • Irises (1890)
  • Olive Trees (1889)

5. Final Months in Auvers-sur-Oise (1890)

Van Gogh painted intensely in his last months. His work from Auvers shows emotional depth and technical mastery.

  • Wheatfield under Thunderclouds (1890)
  • Portrait of Dr. Gachet (1890)
  • Crows over Wheatfield (1890)
  • Church at Auvers (1890)
